About Revision

Revision is all about taking a fresh set of eyes to previously created materials. Although this may sound like glorified proofreading, revision is a whole heap more than that, and to be done effectively, revision requires time, organization, and attention to detail. The danger with revision lies in the temptation to believe you already know all the ins and outs of your work, believing there is nothing left to "do" to it.

Editing is the final step as part of the revision, but revision is not editing. Revision is revisiting, rereading, rethinking, reworking, re-seeing.

Steps for Revision

  1. Start with your feedback. Read through the comments I left on your revised genre analysis draft from Project 1. This is your starting point — not your entire revision plan, but the foundation for one.
  2. Get additional examples if needed. If you need to gather any additional writing samples from your discourse community, do it at the start of the revision (not later).
  3. Create a revision plan. Identify major areas (high/middle-order concerns) first, then minor areas (later-order concerns). What needs the most work? What's a quick fix?
  4. Use your resources. Use the revision resources on this site, visit the Academic Success Center, or review the feedback you've received throughout the semester.
  5. Revise your draft. Expect a non-linear process — you'll find yourself going back and forth between your community samples and your paper, reworking sections, and revisiting earlier decisions. Plan for substantial work.
  6. Proofread. Once you are done with your revision, proofread your paper. You can use tools like the free version of Grammarly (works as a Chrome extension) or GenAI tools like Claude or ChatGPT to help with proofreading. If you use GenAI for proofreading, be cautious with the output to make sure it doesn't strip out your writing voice or change your ideas.

What I'm Looking For

  • Meaningful revision since the revised draft — not just proofreading, but substantive changes that strengthen the paper
  • Well organized with a clear structure
  • Claims are well supported by evidence from your community writing samples
  • Evidence is integrated — samples are woven into your analysis, not just dropped in
  • Readable with clear sentence flow and paragraph structure
  • Generally proofread and cleaned up
  • MLA formatting — in-text citations and Works Cited follow MLA conventions
